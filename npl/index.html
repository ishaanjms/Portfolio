National Physical Laboratory (CSIR-NPL)

A unified digital tool for precise timekeeping and critical
parameters monitoring

Overview

Developed during the research & development internship at the CSIR - National Physical Laboratory under the guidance of Dr. Poonam Arora (Head - Time & Frequency Metrology) , this project replaces a fragmented legacy system with a centralized, real-time monitoring platform. The dashboard tracks critical environmental and instrumental parameters for the CsF1 (Cesium Fountain - 1), the system responsible for maintaining Indian Standard Time (IST).

The Stakes: The CsF1 defines the SI second. Microscopic fluctuations in ambient conditions or laser alignment can introduce measurement errors.

Context &
Challenges

Operating the CsF1 previously required managing isolated and fragile monitoring tools. Data was scattered, and manual oversight risked the stability of national time standards.

Dispersed Data

Essential metrics (Lasers, photodiodes, temp & humidity) were scattered across different hardware controllers.

Manual Monitoring

Scientists had to physically check instruments or log data manually, prone to human error.

Latency

Critical fluctuations in laser alignment needed to be spotted instantly, which legacy systems couldn't do.

Constraints &
Users

The "Dark Room" Constraint

The CsF1 operates in strict laboratory conditions. A primary design constraint was the lighting: the interface could not emit high-luminance white light, as stray photons could interfere with the atomic transition experiment. A custom neon-dark theme was engineered to prevent light pollution.

Designing for Domain Experts

  • Head Scientists: Require immediate oversight of system uptime, operational health, and connection status.
  • Senior Scientists: Rely on live, auto-refreshing graphs to observe the immediate effects of physical interventions (e.g., tweaking laser alignment).
  • PhD Students: Need robust historical analysis tools to download CSV logs for root-cause analysis after overnight failures.

Functional
Requirements

To bridge the gap between the strict lab environment and the scientists' workflow, the digital twin had to fulfill several specific functional criteria.

Live Monitoring & Visualization

  • Real-Time Anomaly Detection: Fetch live data streams to instantly detect anomalies during observations.
  • Parameter Isolation: Provide options to observe a single parameter on a graph, including a rolling feed of the last 30 minutes of behavior.
  • Granular Tracking: Distinct tracking modules separating the "Ambient Lab" (room air) from the "Optical Bench" (table surface).
  • Complex Overlays: Multi-line charts capable of overlaying "Fiber Output," "Grand Detection," and individual "AOM" voltages simultaneously.

Data Management & Accessibility

  • Historical Querying: A dedicated module to query past data by date range and source, with options to plot on-screen or download raw CSVs.
  • Scientific Timestamping: Precise, synchronized time-stamping across multiple formats including IST, MJD, and UTC.
  • Seamless Extraction: Ability to extract the database efficiently without causing system interruptions or latency.
  • Remote Access: Secure remote access to the dashboard via a web link for off-site observation.

System
Architecture

The solution required a ground-up rebuild of the data acquisition pipeline, ensuring isolated hardware could communicate with a single Python backend.

Data Acquisition & Logging

A FLUKE 1620A Thermo-Hygrometer tracks temp/humidity via serial ports, while an ESP8266 microcontroller digitizes 13 channels of laser and photodiode signals, transmitting them wirelessly to a Flask API. Every reading is tagged with localized IST, UTC, and Modified Julian Date (MJD) timestamps to ensure scientific consistency.

Information
Architecture

The system's information architecture is task-oriented, prioritizing immediate real-time oversight while maintaining deep historical accessibility.

The dashboard relies on a persistent sidebar to organize content into logical modules that directly reflect the physical subsystems of the CsF1 atomic clock:

  • Overview: High-level system status aggregating ambient conditions, laser coordinates, and photodiode outputs in a single glance.
  • Temperature & Humidity: Dedicated views comparing the Ambient Lab vs. the Optical Bench, featuring live values and short-term trend lines.
  • Lasers: Grouped X, Y, Z, and D-axis parameters combining live numerical readouts with synchronized time-series plots.
  • Photodiodes: A unified graph overlaying Fiber Output, Grand Detection, and AOM voltages to simplify visual comparisons.
  • Retrieve Data: An isolated module specifically for post-analysis tasks, keeping complex date-pickers and download features away from the live monitoring view.

Visuals &
Typography

A cohesive "Neon-Dark" design language was engineered to meet the strict lighting constraints of the atomic clock laboratory without sacrificing visual clarity.

Neon-Dark Theme

This minimalist and high-contrast theme prioritizes readability for researchers who spend hours analyzing data in a dark environment. The clean interface uses vibrant colors to establish hierarchy against deep-gray background gradients, reducing eye strain.

Color Palette

  • Primary (Background): A dark gradient from `#111111` to `#1A1916` establishes the physical dark-mode requirement while adding subtle depth.
  • Accent (Data Streams): Neon Green (`#21FFAB`), Cyan (`#21DEFF`), and Orange (`#FF5B21`) differentiate complex multi-line graph traces.
  • Highlight (Focus): Coral (`#E26D61`) is used for primary buttons and critical status indicators to draw immediate attention.

Typography

The typeface selection supports quick data consumption, mixing clean sans-serif geometry with technical monospace precision.

  • Roboto: Chosen for all headings and body text for its modern, clean design and high legibility.
  • Fira Code (or equivalent monospace): Used for code snippets and live data readouts, offering excellent numeric character definition and technical clarity.

Interface
Breakdown

The dashboard translates raw CSV logs into an interactive experience using Plotly Dash. Every design decision was rooted in the physical realities of the lab and the specific workflows of the scientists.

Navigation & Macro Overview

To reduce cognitive load, the "Active Tab" is enclosed in a distinct teal background block that registers easily in peripheral vision, so users know where they are in the architecture without reading. Horizontal separators break up the homogeneous icon list, visually dividing passive "Monitoring Tools" (top) from active "Action Tools" (bottom). The Overview screen serves as the starting point, providing a macro-level snapshot of all hardware subsystems and the last server update time.

Temperature & Humidity: Visualizing Thermal Lag

The dashboard breaks the environment into two distinct zones: the "Ambient" room air and the insulated "Optical Bench" where the experiment actually sits. Plotting both temperatures on the same graph allows scientists to visualize "thermal lag." Because the table is insulated, there is a delay before room heat transfers to the equipment. Seeing this correlation lets scientists predict if a temperature shift is about to ruin the laser alignment, giving them time to intervene before the experiment fails.

Lasers: Grouping Opposing Forces

Instead of listing all 8 lasers in a long, random list, the data is grouped into pairs inside dedicated cards (X1 & X2, Y1 & Y2). Because lasers like X1 and X2 are opposing forces that must physically balance each other out, placing them in the same container allows the scientist to instantly compare them. The user can immediately spot an imbalance without having to scan across the whole screen to find the matching partner.

Photodiodes: Troubleshooting the Light Chain

Photodiodes measure light intensity at different stages in a chain: light goes through switches (AOMs), into fibers, and finally to detection. If the final signal dies, the scientist needs to quickly answer: "Is the laser broken, or did just one switch fail?" To make reading the graph effortless, the top row of cards acts as a "living legend." The colored outline and text of the card (e.g., Orange for AOM 5) matches its corresponding trace line exactly on the graph below.

Retrieve Data: Supporting Post-Hoc Analysis

This section was designed strictly to support retrospective analysis. By creating a clear separation between "Quick Plotting" for rapid visual checks and "CSV Export" for deep algorithmic analysis, the interface respects the researcher's workflow. It ensures that the valuable data collected by the system isn't locked inside the dashboard, but remains easily accessible for publication and peer review.
